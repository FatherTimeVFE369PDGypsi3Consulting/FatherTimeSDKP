1Clinical Problem Statement

1.1  The Therapeutic Bottleneck

In clinical orthopedics and neurosurgery, treating deep-tissue infections surrounding the spinal cord and sciatic nerve represents an unresolved crisis. As documented at the International Consensus Meeting on Musculoskeletal Infection (March 12, 2026), chronic device and deep-tissue infections are characterized by dense, mature microbial biofilms that isolate pathogens from both the host immune system and conventional systemic antibiotics. Standard care relies on highly invasive surgical debridement paired with multi-month high-dose antibiotic regimens, with treatment failure rates between 35% and 55%.

Bacterial biofilms secrete a highly structured extracellular polymeric substance (EPS) matrix comprised of polysaccharides, extracellular DNA, and lipids. This matrix drives pathogens into low-metabolic persister states, necessitating antibiotic concentrations up to 1,000× higher than baseline planktonic bactericidal thresholds — concentrations that are severely neurotoxic within the spinal and sciatic nerve microenvironment.

No current therapeutic modality achieves simultaneous blood-nerve barrier penetration, biofilm-specific targeting, biofilm neutralization without cell lysis, and autonomous deactivation without neurotoxic residue in the spinal-sciatic nerve microenvironment.

1.2  Existing State of the Art and Its Limitations

State-of-the-art clinical approaches such as Peptilogics’ engineered anti-biofilm peptide PLG0206 have reduced device-related biofilm failure rates to approximately 7% by mechanically disrupting microbial membranes. However, as noted in synthetic biology literature (February 2026), advanced macrocyclic and self-assembling peptides remain limited by:

  • Erratic structural flexibility under proteolytic challenge from host serum proteases
  • Variable and unpredictable blood-nerve barrier (BNB) penetration
  • Non-specific membrane binding producing off-target demyelination
  • Persistent chemical residue triggering secondary neuroinflammation

The CS-B1 Nano-Peptide addresses each of these limitations through a deterministic, biophysical design framework rather than probabilistic chemical optimization.
